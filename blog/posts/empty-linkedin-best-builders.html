Why The Best Builders Have The Emptiest LinkedIns

A screenshot made the rounds this week. The LinkedIn profile of Apple's incoming CEO.

One job at Apple since 2001. One engineering role before that. No articles. No posts. A default gray placeholder avatar he apparently hadn't bothered to replace. 8,607 followers - the kind of number a mid-career recruiter might have on a slow year. Under "Activity," LinkedIn's own interface tried to apologize for him: "John has no recent posts. Recent posts John shares will be displayed here."

The reaction everywhere was the same word, repeated thousands of times: normalize this.

That reaction says something. Not about one man's profile, but about LinkedIn, and about what developers keep getting told about how to exist on it.

The advice is always the same. Post more. Show up. Write thought leadership. Build your personal brand. Share every launch, every promotion, every half-formed opinion. Be visible. Be active. Be "on the radar." And for years I've quietly suspected that advice has the correlation backwards.

The inverse rule nobody wants to say out loud

The people who post the most on LinkedIn are rarely the people shipping the most.

The ones with the packed Activity feed - six posts a week, ten "repost with a comment" every morning, endless excited to announce and thrilled to share - are almost never the people you'd want building your product. They're selling something. Usually themselves. Sometimes a newsletter, a course, a consulting practice. Very rarely is it software that actually works.

Then there's the other group. Heads down. Shipping. Fixing things. Talking to maybe three people on any given day. Their LinkedIn is a thing they touched in 2019 and forgot about. The photo is outdated. The title is one role behind. The posts don't exist.

The cleanest one-line diagnosis of the whole Ternus screenshot, the take that kept surfacing:

It's almost as if he's actually busy working each day.

That's the whole thing. A VP of Hardware Engineering at Apple for a decade-plus does not need to sell himself. His work sells him. Every Mac review, every iPhone launch, every Apple Silicon benchmark is his résumé. He was part of the team that made it possible for Apple to cut Intel out of the Mac - a thing people said couldn't be done without a generational step back in performance, right up until the M1 shipped and the reviews came in. There is, in fact, more signal about who this person is from ten years of shipped products than any amount of "5 leadership lessons I learned from my morning coffee" could ever generate.

Compare that to someone who's been posting carousel infographics titled "7 things every CTO needs to know" for three years. Which signal would you rather trust?

The obvious counterargument

The pushback always comes fast: LinkedIn got me a job. LinkedIn got me three recruiter calls. LinkedIn pays.

Fair. I'm not arguing LinkedIn is useless. For a lot of people, especially early in their careers, it's a real tool. Recruiters live there. Hiring managers check it. A profile with your current role, a short summary, and some actual work attached is not a bad thing to have. I have one. Most developers should. Having nothing can genuinely hurt you in industries where the hiring motion starts with a LinkedIn search.

What I'm arguing against is different. It's the idea that LinkedIn presence - the posts, the likes, the comments, the "engagement," the performance of having a personal brand - is what makes you a serious professional. That's the lie.

Having a LinkedIn as a lightweight CV that recruiters can pull up is fine. Living on LinkedIn is a red flag. The difference is whether you treat it like a phone book or a stage.

The empty profile isn't a gap. It's the absence of performance. And in a platform that runs almost entirely on performance, absence is a stronger signal than any amount of presence.

The artifact test

Developers have a parallel trap. It's called "build in public."

I've bought into this one - partially. I write blog posts. I share what I learn. Two of my posts have made it to the front page of Hacker News. One got picked up by a newsletter with over a million readers. I'm not pretending I'm above the whole thing.

But I've noticed a difference that doesn't get talked about much. Building in public is not the same as performing work in public.

Building in public is: here's the thing I made, here's what I learned, here's where I got stuck, here's the link to the code. It's specific. It's falsifiable. If I say I built something, you can click on it and see whether I did.

Performing work in public is: "Super proud to announce our latest initiative to empower collaboration across the organization." No artifact. No proof. Just the theater of having done a thing.

The first is useful - to me, to whoever reads it, sometimes to a recruiter who stumbles onto a post. The second is slop. And LinkedIn's feed is tuned to reward the second one, because it costs nothing to produce and keeps people scrolling.

The empty-LinkedIn guy at Apple passes the artifact test without trying. You can hold the artifacts in your hand. The M-series MacBook. The iPad. The Vision Pro. You can't do that with "thought leadership."

For the rest of us, the test is the same, just smaller. Is there something you can point at? A repo? A live site? A post that teaches someone how to do a specific thing? That's the work. Everything else is costume.

None of this means you should delete your LinkedIn. Keep the profile. Update the title when it changes. Add the projects you'd actually brag about to a friend over coffee. Let recruiters find you. That's tool use, and tools are fine.

But the next time I feel that low-grade guilt - I haven't posted anything this week, I'm falling behind, everyone else is out there grinding their personal brand - I'm going to remember the screenshot. One job since 2001. No photo. 8,607 quiet followers. And the verdict the entire internet landed on in the same breath.

Normalize this.

The best builders are usually too busy building.
